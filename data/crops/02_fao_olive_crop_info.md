## Overview
Olive is a major Mediterranean crop adapted to regions with mild, rainy winters and hot, dry summers. It is widely grown for both table olives and oil production.

## Climate Requirements
- Best adapted to Mediterranean climates
- Mild, rainy winter and hot, dry summer are preferred
- A dormancy period of about 2 months with average temperatures below 10°C supports flower bud differentiation
- Short frost periods down to about -6°C can be tolerated during dormancy
- Frost during fruit-bearing damages fruits
- Hot dry winds can reduce fruit setting and cause fruit drop
- High humidity during flowering may cause flower drop and sooty mould

## Soil Requirements
- Can produce acceptable yields even on poor soils if they are:
  - deep
  - well aerated
  - free from waterlogging
- Waterlogging sharply increases oxygen stress and fungal disease risk
- Olive is moderately tolerant to soil salinity
- Preferred salinity: ECe 4.5 mmhos/cm or less
- Upper tolerance limit: about ECe 8 mmhos/cm

## Fertilizer Needs
Typical fertilizer requirements:
- Nitrogen: 200 to 250 kg/ha
- Phosphorus: 55 to 70 kg/ha
- Potassium: 160 to 210 kg/ha

Nitrogen is generally applied before or during flowering and fruit formation.

## Planting and Orchard Notes
- Trees are often raised for 2 years in the nursery before transplanting
- Density may range from:
  - 15 to 20 trees/ha under poor rainfed conditions
  - up to 300 trees/ha under irrigated conditions
- Tree density also depends on pruning strategy
- Intercropping may be used in young orchards but is reduced later

## Water Requirements
- Olive is often grown rainfed where annual rainfall is about 400 to 600 mm
- It may still be found in areas with around 200 mm rainfall
- For high yields, about 600 to 800 mm are needed
- Crop coefficient (Kc) is generally between 0.4 and 0.6

## Critical Water-Sensitive Periods
High yields require adequate water especially:
- from the start of stone hardening
- through yield formation

Additional notes:
- Table olives usually need more water during yield formation than olives grown mainly for oil
- In climates with little winter rain, water may also be needed before flowering
- Water deficit during flowering can increase flower and fruit drop
- Good water supply during active growth helps reduce alternate bearing

## Water Stress and Excess Water
- Spring water deficits reduce leaf development, growth, and yield
- Winter deficits may reduce twig growth, increase defoliation, and delay flowering
- Excess water can cause short twigs, dense foliage, narrow leaves, and reduced yields

## Root and Water Uptake
- Olive develops a strong root system with age
- Water uptake usually occurs over the first 1.2 to 1.7 m of soil depth
- Under ETm of about 5 to 6 mm/day, water uptake starts to decline when around 60 to 70 percent of available soil water has been depleted

## Irrigation Guidance
- With winter rain around 500 mm, irrigation is often applied during and after stone hardening
- With limited winter rain, irrigation may be needed:
  - during bud differentiation
  - before flowering
  - during yield formation
  - especially during stone hardening
- Practical timing examples include:
  - 2 to 3 weeks before flowering
  - when fruit reaches one-third of full size
  - when fruit is almost full size

For oil production:
- irrigation should stop early enough to allow a dry period during ripening

Localized irrigation is preferred when water is limited.

## Harvest and Yield Notes
- Green table olives are commonly harvested from mid-September onward
- Black table olives are commonly harvested in December
- Olives for oil are commonly harvested from mid-December to March
- Maximum oil content and fruit weight are reached about 6 to 8 months after flowering
- Good commercial yields under irrigation are about 50 to 65 kg/tree
- Possible maximum yield may reach about 100 kg/tree
- Oil content of fresh fruit is usually around 20 to 25 percent
