## Overview
Wheat includes bread wheat and durum wheat. It was domesticated in the Near and Middle East and is grown under both rainfed and irrigated conditions across temperate, subtropical, and some tropical highland regions.

## Climate Requirements
- Spring wheat usually matures in about 100 to 130 days
- Winter wheat usually matures in about 180 to 250 days
- Minimum daily temperature for measurable growth: about 5°C
- Optimum mean daily temperature for growth and tillering: 15 to 20°C
- A dry, warm ripening period of about 18°C or more is preferred
- Mean daily temperatures below about 10 to 12°C during the growing season make wheat risky

### Winter Wheat Notes
- Requires a cold period (vernalization) during early growth for normal heading
- In early development it can tolerate frost down to about -20°C
- Frost during active spring growth, heading, or flowering can cause sterility and serious damage

### Spring Wheat Notes
- Does not require chilling for heading
- More suitable in areas with severe winters and little snow
- Still sensitive to frost

## Soil Requirements
- Can grow on a wide range of soils
- Medium-textured soils are preferred
- Peaty soils high in sodium, magnesium, or iron should be avoided
- Optimum soil pH: 6 to 8

## Fertilizer Needs
Typical fertilizer requirements for good yields:
- Nitrogen: up to 150 kg/ha
- Phosphorus: 35 to 45 kg/ha
- Potassium: 25 to 50 kg/ha

## Groundwater and Salinity Notes
- Wheat is relatively tolerant of a high groundwater table
- For sandy loam to silt loam, groundwater depth of about 0.6 to 0.8 m can often be tolerated
- For clay soils, about 0.8 to 1.0 m is usually tolerated
- Long periods with shallow groundwater can reduce yield

### Salinity
- Moderately tolerant to soil salinity
- During germination, ECe should not exceed about 4 mmhos/cm in the upper soil layer
- Yield decreases as salinity rises beyond suitable thresholds

## Planting Notes
- With pre-irrigation or sufficient rainfall, seeds are usually drilled 2 to 4 cm deep
- In dry soils, deeper sowing of 5 to 8 cm may be used
- Under good water supply and fertilization, row spacing is often 0.12 to 0.15 m
- Under poor rainfall conditions, row spacing may increase to 0.25 m or more
- Typical sowing rates under irrigation:
  - 100 to 120 kg/ha when drilled
  - 110 to 140 kg/ha when broadcast
- Wheat is commonly grown in rotation with legumes, sunflower, and maize

## Water Requirements
- For high yields, total water requirement is about 450 to 650 mm depending on climate and growth duration

### Typical Crop Coefficients (Kc)
- Initial stage: 0.3 to 0.4
- Development stage: 0.7 to 0.8
- Mid-season: 1.05 to 1.2
- Late season: 0.65 to 0.7
- At harvest: 0.2 to 0.25

## Critical Water-Sensitive Periods
Wheat is especially sensitive to water deficit during:
- late tillering / early elongation, when heads and potential seeds are being determined
- heading and flowering, when seed number per head is strongly affected
- early yield formation, when grain filling can be reduced and shrivelling may occur under hot, dry winds

Important note:
- Water stress during flowering is especially damaging and later irrigation cannot fully recover the yield loss

## Water Stress and Yield Effects
- Slight deficits during the vegetative period may have limited effect and may sometimes hasten maturation
- Severe deficit during flowering reduces head development, grain number, and final yield
- Deficit during yield formation reduces grain weight and quality
- Water deficit during ripening has only a slight effect on yield compared with earlier stages

## Root and Water Uptake
- Wheat first develops a primary root system, then a fibrous root system
- Active rooting depth:
  - Spring wheat: about 0.9 m, maximum about 1.2 to 1.5 m
  - Winter wheat: up to about 1.2 m active depth, with deeper maximum rooting possible
- Most water uptake occurs in the upper soil layers:
  - 50 to 60 percent from the first 0.3 m
  - 20 to 25 percent from the second 0.3 m
  - 10 to 15 percent from the third 0.3 m
- Normally, nearly all water uptake occurs over the first 1.0 to 1.5 m

## Irrigation Guidance
- For non-stress conditions, about 50 to 60 percent of available soil water can usually be depleted before the next irrigation
- Filling the root zone to field capacity before or soon after sowing supports better root development
- Overwatering during the vegetative stage can cause excessive growth and lodging
- With limited water, irrigation should be timed to avoid deficit during flowering and early grain formation

### Practical Rule
Where rainfall is low and water is limited, prioritize irrigation:
- before or near sowing if needed
- around flowering
- during early yield formation

## Yield Notes
- A good commercial grain yield under irrigation is about 6 to 9 tons/ha
- Water use efficiency for grain is about 0.8 to 1.6 kg/m3
