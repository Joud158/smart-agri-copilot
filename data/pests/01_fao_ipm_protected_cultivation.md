## Overview
Integrated pest management (IPM) in greenhouses combines cultural, physical, mechanical, biological, and chemical methods to manage pests and diseases while reducing routine pesticide use. Early detection, sanitation, and consistent monitoring are the foundation of an effective greenhouse IPM programme.

## Core IPM Principle
Greenhouse pest management should focus on:
- prevention first
- early detection
- accurate identification
- timely intervention
- minimizing routine pesticide dependence

## Pest Exclusion and Greenhouse Hygiene

### Sanitation
- Keep the inside and outside of the greenhouse free from weeds and alternative host plants
- Remove high-risk plants near the greenhouse that can harbour pests
- Clean up crop refuse regularly
- Remove infected leaves and severely diseased plants quickly

### Airlock Entrance
- An airlock entrance reduces pest entry through walk-in doors
- A double-door system is especially important in fan-and-pad systems
- The entrance area can also be used for sanitation practices such as footbaths and hand washing

### Insect Screening
- Fine-mesh screens help exclude pests from greenhouse openings
- Screens reduce airflow, so ventilation design must compensate for the resistance
- Screening is useful in both passively ventilated and mechanically ventilated greenhouses

### Reflective or Metallized Mulches
- Reflective mulches reduce entry of whiteflies and thrips
- Combining screens with reflective mulch gives stronger exclusion than either method alone

## Monitoring and Scouting

### Core Monitoring Rule
Even with exclusion methods, pests will still enter, so early monitoring is essential.

### Recommended Methods
- yellow sticky traps
- blue sticky traps
- plant scouting
- hand lens inspection
- checking hotspots near vents and doors

### Sticky Trap Guidance
- Blue sticky cards are useful for thrips
- Yellow sticky cards are useful for whiteflies and other small flying insects
- A common guidance range is 1 to 4 cards per 100 m2
- Additional cards should be placed near vents and doorways
- Cards should be inspected and replaced weekly

### Plant Inspection Guidance
- Inspect plants regularly in a zigzag path through the greenhouse
- Check the underside of leaves
- Look for immature whiteflies, mites, aphids, disease symptoms, and root health problems
- Monitor ten locations per 100 m2 where possible

### Record-Keeping
Monitoring records should include:
- pest or disease observed
- approximate population level
- location in greenhouse
- crop area affected
- action taken
- whether the previous intervention worked

## Soil Preparation and Plant Health Basics

### Soil Management
- Slightly acidic soil around pH 6.5 is preferred
- Soil testing should be used when pH or fertility is uncertain
- Lime can raise pH
- Sulphur can lower pH
- Regular addition of compost or organic matter supports biologically active soil

### Plant Selection
- Choose resistant cultivars whenever possible
- Purchase healthy, certified transplants or grow from tested seed
- Avoid overcrowding because weak growth and poor airflow increase pest and disease risk
- Avoid plant injury because wounds increase infection risk

## Humidity and Disease Prevention

### Why Humidity Matters
High relative humidity is one of the main drivers of greenhouse disease problems, especially Botrytis and other foliar diseases.

### Humidity Reduction Practices
- heat and vent in the evening and early morning when needed
- use horizontal airflow (HAF) fans where available
- avoid overwatering
- maintain proper plant spacing when possible
- prevent dense overgrown canopies through proper nutrition and plant management

## Resistant Cultivars
Using resistant cultivars is often one of the most effective disease-management tools in greenhouse IPM. However:
- growers may need multiple resistance traits
- new cultivars should be tested on a small scale before full adoption
- market acceptance still matters alongside disease resistance

## Growing Media and Damping-Off Prevention
- Soilless media may need preventive fungicidal or biological treatment in susceptible crops
- Field soil used in media must be treated to eliminate pathogens, insects, and weed seeds
- Once treated, media should be protected from reinfestation

## Main Disease Rules

### General Disease Management
Disease management should combine:
- resistant cultivars
- sanitation
- sound cultural practices
- humidity control
- proper fungicide or biofungicide use when needed

### Late Blight
Favouring conditions:
- relative humidity 90 to 100 percent
- temperature roughly 3 to 26°C
- optimum around 18 to 22°C

Main management:
- remove debris
- reduce humidity and leaf wetness
- improve airflow
- prevent overly lush canopy growth

### Early Blight
Favouring conditions:
- warmth and humidity
- heavy fruit load
- crowded plants
- dew and shading

Main management:
- sanitation
- rotation where possible
- reduce humidity and leaf wetness
- avoid overly dense canopy growth

### Botrytis Blight
Favouring conditions:
- free moisture for 8 to 12 hours
- relative humidity at least 90 percent
- temperature around 13 to 18°C

Main management:
- remove infected tissue
- reduce humidity and leaf wetness duration
- improve airflow
- prevent excessive vegetative growth

### Powdery Mildew
Favouring conditions:
- very high relative humidity
- moderate temperatures around 20 to 30°C
- lower light conditions

Main management:
- maintain proper spacing
- keep relative humidity below about 90 percent
- heat and vent when needed
- remove weed hosts and crop residues

### Downy Mildew
Favouring conditions:
- around 6 hours of saturated leaf surface humidity
- temperature roughly 5 to 30°C
- optimum around 15 to 20°C

Main management:
- humidity control
- sound cultural practices
- fungicides where needed

### Damping-Off
Common causal organisms include:
- Rhizoctonia
- Fusarium
- Phytophthora
- Pythium

Main prevention:
- certified disease-free seed
- treated seed where appropriate
- pasteurized or clean media
- avoid overwatering
- avoid overcrowding
- provide adequate light and airflow

## Bacterial Disease Rules

### General Management
Bacterial diseases are commonly introduced by:
- infected seed
- infected transplants
- crop residues
- weeds
- tools and handling

Main GAP-style management:
- use certified seed
- remove infected plants quickly
- minimize plant handling
- reduce leaf wetness
- irrigate early so foliage dries quickly
- maintain good ventilation
- disinfect benches, tools, flats, and stakes
- use copper preventively where appropriate

## Viral Disease Rules

### Main Principle
Infected plants cannot be cured. Control focuses on:
- exclusion
- prevention
- vector control
- weed host control
- resistant or tolerant varieties

### Practical Virus Management
- use virus-free seed and transplants
- avoid overlapping crops where possible
- destroy old crops promptly
- remove weed hosts
- manage vectors such as whiteflies, aphids, and thrips
- use reflective mulches and strategic insect protection where appropriate

## General Pest Monitoring Guidance

### Key Pests Mentioned
- aphids
- whiteflies
- thrips
- spider mites
- broad mites
- cyclamen mites
- tomato borer
- damping-off organisms
- common fungal and bacterial diseases

### Example Monitoring Logic
- aphids: inspect young growth, honeydew, ants, curled leaves
- whiteflies: inspect underside of upper leaves and use yellow traps
- thrips: inspect sticky cards and feeding scars on foliage
- spider mites: inspect underside of leaves in hot, dry zones
- tomato borer: use pheromone traps and check for mines

## Biological Control
Biological control is especially useful when introduced early, while pest numbers are still low.

Examples mentioned in the source include:
- parasitoids and predators for aphids
- predatory mites for spider mites and thrips
- parasitoids for whiteflies
- Bacillus thuringiensis for caterpillars
- Beauveria bassiana for some insect pests
- biofungicides such as Bacillus subtilis and Trichoderma for some diseases

## Core Best Practices
- scout weekly
- keep sanitation strict
- reduce humidity and leaf wetness
- maintain airflow
- remove weed hosts
- use resistant cultivars where possible
- intervene early, not late
- use biological control before pest populations explode
- rotate pesticides if chemical control is required
