## Overview
Integrated pest management (IPM) is a broad crop-protection framework that combines multiple strategies to grow healthy crops while reducing unnecessary pesticide use. In greenhouse systems, IPM is especially important because enclosed conditions can favour rapid pest and disease development if prevention and monitoring are weak.

## FAO IPM Definition
IPM is an ecosystem-based approach to crop production and protection that combines different management strategies and practices to grow healthy crops and minimize pesticide use.

## Why IPM Matters in Greenhouses
Greenhouse conditions can favour pests and diseases because:
- crops are enclosed
- temperature and humidity may be close to ideal for pest development
- there is no rain or wind to disrupt populations
- monoculture is common
- crop rotation is often limited
- selected high-yielding varieties may be more vulnerable in some cases

At the same time, greenhouse stability also makes some non-chemical controls more practical, especially biological control and exclusion methods.

## Main IPM Control Categories
The chapter describes these major control groups:
- cultural control
- host plant resistance
- biological control
- other biologically based control such as pheromones and plant extracts
- mechanical control such as nets, sticky bands, and traps
- physical control such as solar heat treatment
- chemical control

## Core IPM Logic
A successful greenhouse IPM programme should:
- prevent pest problems where possible
- monitor crops and detect problems early
- identify the pest or disease correctly
- choose the least risky effective intervention
- avoid routine dependence on pesticides alone
- integrate multiple control methods rather than using only one

## IPM in Protected Cultivation
Greenhouse crop protection requires a multifaceted approach because:
- crop schedules often overlap
- worker movement can spread pests and pathogens
- soil-borne problems may persist where rotation is weak
- high crop value can justify intensive control measures
- chemical treatments need extra care because of phytotoxicity and residue concerns

Some infestation can sometimes be tolerated in food crops, but tolerance is much lower when market quality standards are strict.

## Nursery and Planting Material Principles
Before production starts:
- use healthy, certified seed or transplants
- keep nursery hygiene strict
- avoid introducing infected or infested planting material
- inspect young plants before transfer to production areas
- isolate suspicious material rather than mixing it with clean batches

## Pest and Disease Management Before Planting
Before planting a new crop:
- destroy residues from the previous crop immediately after final harvest
- clean the greenhouse thoroughly before starting again
- remove roots, plant debris, and weeds that can harbour pests and pathogens
- wash soil and debris from tools and equipment before moving between greenhouses
- disinfect or thoroughly clean greenhouse structures
- use insect-proof screening on openings where appropriate
- use an entry SAS / airlock with sanitation measures where possible

## Soil and Pre-Plant Measures
When needed:
- use solarization or soil disinfection during the crop-free period
- manage soil-borne pests and nematodes before planting
- consider soilless systems if repeated soil problems cannot be reduced to manageable levels

## Pest and Disease Management During the Crop Cycle
During production:
- use cultural practices that support natural prevention
- manage irrigation carefully
- use balanced fertilization
- maintain good sanitation during the crop cycle
- avoid creating conditions that favour outbreaks
- reduce unnecessary overlap between old and new crops
- manage worker movement carefully so pests are not carried from infested houses to clean ones

## Monitoring and Decision-Making
Monitoring is essential for IPM success.

A practical monitoring system should include:
- regular crop inspection
- pest and disease identification
- population tracking
- hotspot detection
- records of observations and interventions
- follow-up evaluation of whether a treatment worked

Key decision questions include:
- What organism is present?
- Is it likely to cause economic or agronomic damage?
- What non-chemical options are available?
- Are biological, physical, or cultural measures sufficient?
- If pesticides are needed, how can they be used intelligently and safely?

## Host Plant Resistance
Host plant resistance is an important IPM tool.

Good practice:
- choose resistant cultivars or rootstocks when possible
- match resistance choice to the likely pest or disease pressure
- do not rely on resistance alone
- combine resistance with hygiene, monitoring, and sound crop management

## Biological Control
Biological control uses living organisms such as:
- insect predators
- mite predators
- parasitoids
- nematode antagonists
- microbial agents such as bacteria, fungi, and viruses

Greenhouses can be especially suitable for biological control because the environment is relatively stable and introductions can be managed more precisely.

## Mechanical and Physical Control
Important non-chemical tools include:
- insect nets and screens
- sticky traps
- colour traps
- light traps
- sanitation barriers
- solar heat or solarization for pest suppression in soil or substrates

## Chemical Control in IPM
Chemical control is part of IPM, but not the only tool.

Good chemical-control practice means:
- use it only when justified
- choose products carefully
- avoid unnecessary prophylactic use
- consider phytotoxicity risk
- consider residue risk
- rotate modes of action where needed
- integrate with other controls rather than replacing them

## Worker Training and Hygiene
Greenhouse workers are part of the pest-management system.

Training should cover:
- hygiene rules
- movement between greenhouses
- recognition of early pest and disease symptoms
- reporting procedures
- safe handling of pesticides and sanitation products
- consistent implementation of monitoring and control routines

## GAP-Style Best Practices
A strong greenhouse IPM programme should include:
- clean planting material
- strict sanitation before planting
- greenhouse exclusion methods
- monitoring and record-keeping
- sound irrigation and fertilization
- balanced use of cultural, biological, mechanical, physical, and chemical controls
- careful worker training and movement rules
- review and adjustment of the programme over time
