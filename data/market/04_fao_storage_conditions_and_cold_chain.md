## Overview
Proper storage is essential to maintain product quality after harvest. Good storage begins with high-quality produce, sound containers, and correct environmental control. The main storage variables are:
- temperature
- relative humidity
- air circulation
- ventilation
- compatibility between stored commodities

## Core Storage Rules
General good storage practice includes:
- starting with produce that is not damaged or diseased
- using well-ventilated containers strong enough for stacking
- controlling temperature
- controlling relative humidity
- maintaining air circulation
- leaving space between containers for adequate ventilation
- avoiding incompatible product mixes

## Ethylene Compatibility
Products stored together should tolerate the same:
- temperature
- relative humidity
- ethylene level

Important rule:
- high ethylene producers can damage ethylene-sensitive products

Examples from the source:
- high ethylene producers include ripe bananas, apples, and cantaloupe
- ethylene-sensitive commodities include lettuce, cucumbers, carrots, potatoes, and sweet potatoes

## Temperature Management Principles
Storage temperature can be improved by:
- shading buildings
- using reflective white or silver exterior surfaces
- using evaporative cooling methods where suitable
- locating facilities at higher altitudes where ambient temperature is lower
- using underground or partly underground structures where practical

General design note:
- square buildings are more efficient to cool than rectangular buildings because they have less wall area per unit of storage space

## Relative Humidity Principles
Different products need different RH levels.

Important examples:
- onions and garlic store better at lower relative humidity
- most fresh vegetables need much higher RH to limit water loss
- products suited to dry storage should be protected from floor dampness

## Basic Storage Practices
Routine storage management should include:
- inspecting produce regularly
- cleaning storage structures regularly
- removing spoiled or infected produce immediately
- disinfecting reusable sacks or containers before reuse
- keeping the surrounding area free from weeds and trash
- protecting facilities from rodents
- using pallets, poles, or waterproof sheets to keep produce off damp floors

## Ventilation Principles
Good ventilation helps:
- extend shelf life
- reduce disease spread
- maintain temperature uniformity
- prevent moisture problems

Important design rules:
- air inlets should be low
- air outlets should be high
- floor vents and lateral ducts improve air movement
- even loading is important in bulk storage to avoid dead zones

## Insulation and Cooling Structures
Insulation improves storage performance by reducing heat gain and energy use.

Possible storage approaches include:
- insulated refrigerated rooms
- evaporatively cooled rooms
- night-ventilated stores
- underground or semi-underground storage
- root cellars
- barrel or pit storage for small quantities
- crop-specific structures such as yam barns or potato storage clamps

## Root and Tuber Crop Notes
Important rules for root and tuber crops:
- potatoes for processing are stored at intermediate temperatures to reduce sugar formation that can darken during heating
- potatoes for consumption should be stored in the dark to avoid greening and solanine formation
- tropical root and tuber crops must be protected from chilling injury

## Controlled Atmosphere (CA) Storage
Controlled or modified atmosphere storage should supplement, not replace, good temperature and RH management.

Possible atmosphere-control methods include:
- lowering oxygen
- increasing or lowering carbon dioxide
- reducing ethylene using scrubbers such as potassium permanganate or activated charcoal

Main rule:
- CA storage is an advanced option, not a substitute for basic storage discipline

## Relative Perishability
The source classifies horticultural products by perishability.

Important examples:
- very high perishability: ripe tomato and many leafy vegetables
- high perishability: eggplant, pepper, partially ripe tomato
- moderate perishability: immature potato
- low perishability: mature potato, dry onion, garlic, pumpkin, winter squash, sweet potato, taro, yam

## Practical System Logic
The advisor should use storage reasoning like this:
- check crop type
- check maturity stage
- check recommended temperature and RH
- check ethylene sensitivity
- avoid incompatible storage mixes
- warn about chilling risk where relevant
- distinguish short-life products from long-storage products
