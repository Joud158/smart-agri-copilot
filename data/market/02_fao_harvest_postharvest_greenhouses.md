## Overview
Post-harvest quality is central to profitability. Greenhouse crops are grown not only to produce food, but to deliver a product that satisfies buyers and consumers repeatedly. Quality at harvest and along the supply chain depends on correct harvest timing, careful handling, temperature management, sanitation, packaging, storage, transport, and traceability.

## What Quality Means
Quality depends on the market and the buyer, but key quality dimensions include:
- external appearance: size, weight, colour, shape, gloss, firmness, freshness
- internal quality: texture, taste, aroma, composition, nutritional value
- freedom from defects: bruises, cuts, scars, rots, insect and disease damage
- food safety: absence of toxins, contaminants, pathogens, and unacceptable residues

General rule:
- consumers often buy first with their eyes
- repeat purchases depend on internal eating quality and consistency

## Pre-Harvest Factors Affecting Post-Harvest Quality
Post-harvest quality is influenced by:
- planting material quality
- growing conditions
- substrate type
- nutrient management
- pest and disease management
- temperature, water, and light during growth

Important note:
- good post-harvest handling can maintain quality, but it cannot improve poor quality created before harvest

## Harvest Maturity Principles
Each crop has an optimum harvest stage depending on:
- crop type
- cultivar
- distance to market
- transport time
- intended use

General rules:
- harvesting too early can cause poor flavour, greater water loss, more injury, and more disorders
- harvesting too late can cause softness, rapid senescence, decay risk, and loss of texture
- harvest stage should be chosen so the product reaches the consumer at optimum eating quality

## Crop-Specific Harvest Notes

### Bell Pepper
- Usually harvested at full size
- Harvest colour depends on the target market
- Fruit should be uniform in shape and free of defects such as blossom end rot, cracking, decay, or sunburn

### Tomato
- Tomato is climacteric and can ripen after harvest
- Fruit harvested at mature green or breaker stage can ripen later, but usually has lower sweetness and flavour than fruit harvested at more advanced stages
- Nearby markets allow harvest at more mature stages
- Distant export markets require earlier harvest stages

Useful tomato maturity sequence:
- green
- breaker
- turning
- pink
- light red
- red

### Cucumber
- Harvested while physiologically immature but edible
- Best harvested near full size before seeds become full size or hard
- Good indicators include firmness, glossiness, dark green colour, and absence of yellowing

### Green Bean
- Harvest while pods are bright green, fleshy, tender, and seeds are still small
- Pods should snap easily and be free of bulges from enlarged seeds

### Eggplant
- Usually harvested immature, before seeds enlarge and harden
- Important quality signs: typical size, shape, colour, and a fresh green calyx

### Melon
- Correct harvest stage depends on cultivar
- Some cultivars such as cantaloupe are harvested at about three-quarter to full slip
- Honeydew maturity is often judged by ground-colour change rather than slip

### Lettuce
- Often harvested before full hard-head development in greenhouse systems
- Leaves should have typical cultivar colour, be unwilted, and free from defects
- In some systems whole plants are harvested with roots attached

## Harvesting Operations
Good harvesting practice requires:
- planning harvest according to market and packing strategy
- training staff to recognize correct maturity
- harvesting in the early morning when temperatures are lower
- using clean, sharp, sanitized tools
- gentle handling to avoid bruises, cuts, and scarring
- immediate shading after harvest
- very short delay between harvest and first cooling

Important rule:
- physical damage increases water loss, pathogen entry, ethylene production, and post-harvest rots

## Core Harvest GAP Rules
- harvest at the maturity needed for optimum consumer quality
- avoid harvesting too early or too late
- prevent physical damage
- sanitize harvest tools regularly
- move product quickly from the greenhouse to the packing/cooling area

## Temperature Management
Two main factors largely determine post-harvest quality:
- physical damage
- temperature management

The two main parts of temperature management are:
- precooling to remove field heat quickly
- storage at the correct temperature during accumulation, transport, and distribution

Lowering product temperature:
- reduces respiration
- slows ripening
- reduces ethylene effects
- reduces wilting
- slows decay development

## Precooling Methods

### Passive Room Cooling
- Uses a regular cool room
- Slower and less effective for rapid field-heat removal
- Better suited for storage than for fast initial cooling

### Forced Air Cooling
- Faster than passive room cooling
- Often the most practical and cost-effective method for common greenhouse crops
- Cold air is forced through packed produce

Advantages:
- shorter time at warm temperatures
- efficient cooling
- works with many container types if ventilation openings are adequate

### Vacuum Cooling
- Best for leafy crops with high surface-to-volume ratio
- Very fast
- Often used for lettuce and similar leafy vegetables
- Expensive equipment

### Hydrocooling
- Uses cold sanitized water
- Faster than forced air for some products
- Useful for certain vegetables such as cucumbers and beans
- Requires clean, sanitized water and suitable containers

### Ice Cooling
- Can cool product quickly
- Usually not preferred because it is expensive and may increase rot risk as ice melts

## Cooling GAP Rules
- harvest early, not during peak heat
- shade harvested produce immediately
- cool as soon as possible after harvest
- forced-air cooling is often the best general option
- avoid chilling temperatures in chill-sensitive crops

## Storage Conditions
Each crop has an optimum temperature and high relative humidity is generally needed to reduce water loss.

General rule:
- relative humidity in cool stores should usually remain above 90 percent for greenhouse vegetables

Examples from the chapter:
- bell pepper: about 7–13°C
- cucumber: about 10–13°C
- eggplant: about 8–12°C
- lettuce: about 0°C
- tomato mature green: about 13–21°C
- tomato firm red: about 8–10°C

## Chilling Injury
Most greenhouse vegetables in this chapter, except lettuce, are susceptible to chilling injury if stored too cold.

Chilling injury may appear as:
- pitting
- browning
- sunken tissue
- water-soaked lesions
- poor coloration
- uneven softening
- decay after return to warmer temperatures

Main rule:
- store above the chilling threshold for each crop

## Moisture Loss
Greenhouse vegetables contain very high water content, so water loss directly reduces saleable value and quality.

Water loss causes:
- loss of firmness
- loss of crispness
- shrinkage
- reduced visual quality

To reduce moisture loss:
- maintain high relative humidity
- avoid mechanical damage
- use proper packaging
- keep product cool

## Ethylene
Ethylene is a natural plant hormone and gas that can either help ripening or accelerate deterioration.

Important effects:
- can induce premature ripening
- can increase senescence
- can speed deterioration

Important examples:
- tomato responds strongly to ethylene and can be ripened with it
- cucumber is highly sensitive
- lettuce may develop browning
- peppers respond less strongly than tomatoes

### Reducing Harmful Ethylene Effects
- avoid exhaust pollution near facilities
- remove decaying products quickly
- avoid physical damage
- use electric forklifts in packhouses and coolstores
- use ethylene scrubbers if needed
- maintain proper storage temperatures
- use 1-MCP where permitted and useful

## Sorting and Grading
After harvest, produce should be sorted and graded according to:
- market requirements
- export and phytosanitary standards
- size
- colour
- defects
- shape
- quality uniformity

Important practice:
- remove rejects quickly because they can become sources of pathogens and ethylene

## Post-Harvest Pathogens and Decay
Most post-harvest decay develops when produce has:
- been stressed during growth
- been physically damaged
- been stored too long
- been exposed to chilling injury

Decay prevention depends on:
- sanitation
- gentle handling
- rapid cooling
- correct storage temperature
- approved and careful use of sanitizers or post-harvest treatments where allowed

## Sanitation and Food Safety
Sanitation is essential throughout:
- greenhouse
- packhouse
- grading line
- coolstores
- transport vehicles
- markets

Good sanitation may include:
- potable or sanitized wash water
- approved sanitizing agents
- regular cleaning of equipment and surfaces
- strict worker hygiene
- HACCP-style control where relevant

## Packaging
Packaging has three main functions:
- protect product from physical and pathological damage
- assemble products in manageable units
- present the product with branding and traceability

Good packaging should:
- be strong enough for stacking
- allow ventilation during cooling and storage
- meet market requirements
- preferably be recyclable or returnable

## Traceability
Traceability is increasingly important for:
- food safety
- biosecurity
- brand protection
- inventory control
- logistics management

Useful systems include:
- barcodes
- QR codes
- RFID
- temperature and condition monitoring along the chain

## Transport to Market
To maintain quality:
- use refrigerated transport for longer or warmer journeys
- load from cooled areas when possible
- keep airflow through pallets uniform
- avoid delays
- maintain the cold chain from packhouse to market

## Supply Chain Perspective
Successful post-harvest management requires coordination across the full chain:
- harvest
- cooling
- temporary storage
- transport
- destination handling
- retail display
- consumer use

Main rule:
- product movement should be smooth, fast, cool, and coordinated

## Staff Training
Post-harvest success depends heavily on trained personnel.

Staff should understand:
- maturity standards
- hygiene
- careful handling
- cooling rules
- sorting and grading expectations
- traceability and quality procedures

## Core Best Practices
- harvest at the right maturity
- handle gently
- cool quickly
- store at the right temperature and humidity
- avoid chilling injury
- reduce moisture loss
- control ethylene exposure
- keep sanitation strict
- grade for uniformity
- use protective and ventilated packaging
- maintain traceability
- protect the cold chain through transport
- train staff consistently
