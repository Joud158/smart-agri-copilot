## Overview
Water quality is a major factor in greenhouse horticulture, especially where water supplies are limited and growers may need to use lower-quality water. The chapter focuses on the main water quality parameters affecting greenhouse production, especially pH, alkalinity, salinity, and specific ion toxicity.

## Water Sources
Irrigation water may come from:
- surface water
- groundwater
- wastewater after treatment

In Mediterranean coastal areas, groundwater may have elevated dissolved salts, and wastewater may carry additional health and management risks.

## Main Water Quality Categories
Water quality parameters can be grouped into:
- physical
- chemical
- biological

## Physical Parameters

### Temperature
- Irrigation water should be close to plant and root-zone temperature
- Cold water can reduce root activity and nutrient uptake
- Very warm water can also be harmful
- Water above about 35°C can damage plants

### Suspended Solids
Suspended solids may include:
- soil particles
- industrial particles
- partially treated wastewater solids
- organic matter

Main risks:
- clogging of irrigation equipment
- abrasion and wear
- reduced distribution efficiency
- more maintenance
- indirect crop stress from blocked emitters

Localized irrigation systems such as drip systems are especially sensitive to suspended solids.

### Blockage Risk Guidance
General clogging risk for localized irrigation:
- suspended solids below 50 mg/L: low restriction
- 50–100 mg/L: slight to moderate restriction
- above 100 mg/L: severe restriction

## Chemical Parameters

### pH
- pH expresses acidity or basicity of water
- Neutral pH is 7
- General irrigation water range often considered acceptable: about 6.0 to 8.5
- Optimum irrigation water pH is commonly around 6.5 to 7.5
- pH below 5 or above 8.5 is considered abnormal for irrigation use

Important notes:
- abnormal pH may indicate other water-quality problems
- low-pH water may damage roots
- abnormal pH may corrode pipelines, sprinklers, and control equipment
- irrigation water pH can gradually influence substrate or soil pH over time

### Alkalinity
Alkalinity is not the same as pH.

- pH measures acidity/basicity at the moment
- alkalinity measures the water’s capacity to resist pH change or raise growing-media pH over time

Main contributors:
- bicarbonates
- carbonates
- hydroxyl ions

Important rules:
- high alkalinity tends to raise the pH of growing media over time
- water with alkalinity above about 100 ppm CaCO3 should make the grower consider acid-type soluble fertilizers rather than calcium-based fertilizers
- acid injection is commonly used to manage high-alkalinity water

Example:
- water around 400 ppm CaCO3 alkalinity can strongly increase growing-media pH over time

## Salinity

### General Meaning
Salinity reflects the concentration of dissolved mineral salts in water.

Common ions involved:
- sodium
- calcium
- magnesium
- potassium
- chloride
- sulphate
- bicarbonate
- carbonate
- nitrate

### Why Salinity Matters
Poor-quality saline water can harm the soil-water-plant relationship and reduce crop productivity.

Three major salinity-related problems:
- osmotic stress, making water uptake harder
- effects on soil or substrate chemistry and structure
- phytotoxicity from specific ions

### Salinity Measurement
Salinity may be expressed as:
- total dissolved salts / total dissolved solids
- electrical conductivity (EC)

Key rules:
- water is considered brackish at about 2 g/L total dissolved salts or more
- water is also considered brackish at about EC 3.0 dS/m or more
- a practical conversion often used is:
  - TDS (ppm) ≈ EC (mS/cm) × 640

## Specific Ion Hazards

### Sodium
- can build up in soil or substrate
- may worsen soil physical properties
- may become toxic at high concentration

### Chloride
- needed only in small amounts
- may accumulate and become toxic
- trees, vines, and woody crops are generally more sensitive than many annual crops

General chloride hazard guidance:
- below about 70 ppm: generally safe
- 71–140 ppm: slight to moderate injury possible in sensitive plants
- 140–350 ppm: injury risk rises for moderately tolerant plants
- above 350 ppm: severe problems likely

### Boron
- essential at low concentration
- toxic at relatively low concentration

General guidance:
- 0.2–0.5 mg/L may occur normally in irrigation water
- above about 0.3 mg/L may harm sensitive crops
- above about 4.0 mg/L is unsuitable for almost all crops

Symptoms of boron toxicity may appear first on older leaves as:
- yellowing
- chlorotic spots
- dried tissue at tips and edges

Seedlings are often more sensitive than mature plants.

### Iron and Manganese
Excessive concentrations may:
- create precipitates
- block equipment
- stain leaves
- reduce crop quality

### Carbonates and Bicarbonates
- contribute to alkalinity
- can raise soil or substrate pH
- can form limescale with calcium and magnesium
- may cause pipe and nozzle deposits

## Water Testing Before Site Selection
Appropriate water-quality testing should be done before selecting a greenhouse site.

Important analytical parameters include:
- pH
- EC / salinity
- total dissolved solids
- calcium
- magnesium
- sodium
- chlorides
- bicarbonates / carbonates
- sulphates
- boron
- iron
- manganese

## Management and Correction Measures
Depending on the problem, management may include:
- filtration for suspended solids
- acid injection for high alkalinity
- choosing acid-type fertilizers where alkalinity is high
- careful equipment choice for abnormal pH water
- avoiding highly saline or toxic-ion water where possible
- using agronomic strategies when poor-quality water must be used
