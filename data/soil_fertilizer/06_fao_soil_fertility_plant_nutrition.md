## Overview
Soil fertility is the ability of soil to provide nutrients for crop production from both its own reserves and external inputs. In greenhouse conditions, fertility management is especially important because high temperatures and intensive agronomic inputs can accelerate changes in biological, chemical, and physical soil properties.

## Soil Fertility Factors
Greenhouse soil fertility depends on the interaction of:
- biological factors, such as microbial populations, beneficial fungi, and pathogens
- physical factors, such as soil texture, structure, rootable volume, and water-holding capacity
- chemical factors, such as nutrient status, soil organic matter, soil pH, and cation exchange capacity

## Soil Texture and Structure
Soil texture describes the relative proportion of sand, silt, and clay particles.
Soil structure describes how particles aggregate and how pores are arranged.

These directly influence:
- water movement
- aeration
- root growth
- nutrient availability
- biological activity

## Soil Organic Matter (SOM)
Soil organic matter plays an important role in:
- nutrient release
- energy supply for soil microorganisms
- nutrient exchange capacity
- soil porosity and aeration
- water-holding capacity
- limiting compaction and erosion

Approximate SOM interpretation ranges:
- sandy soils: medium around 1.5–2.0%
- loamy soils: medium around 1.9–2.5%
- clay/silty soils: medium around 2.3–3.0%

## Soil pH
Soil pH is a master variable because it controls many chemical processes and strongly affects nutrient availability.

General rule:
- most plants perform best around pH 6 to 7.5

Important notes:
- pH affects nutrient chemical form
- pH also influences microbial activity
- high or low pH can restrict nutrient uptake even when nutrients are present

## Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)
CEC expresses the maximum quantity of cations that a soil can hold for exchange with the soil solution.

General interpretation:
- low: < 10 meq+/100 g
- medium: 10–20 meq+/100 g
- high: > 20 meq+/100 g

Higher CEC generally means:
- better nutrient retention
- better buffering capacity
- lower risk of cation loss

## Essential Plant Nutrients
Plants need 16 essential inorganic elements.

### Main macronutrients
- Nitrogen (N)
- Phosphorus (P)
- Potassium (K)
- Sulphur (S)
- Calcium (Ca)
- Magnesium (Mg)

### Main micronutrients
- Iron (Fe)
- Manganese (Mn)
- Zinc (Zn)
- Copper (Cu)
- Boron (B)
- Molybdenum (Mo)
- Chlorine (Cl)

In greenhouse production, the main fertilization focus is often on:
- N
- P
- K

because Ca, Mg, and S may sometimes already be available from soil and irrigation water.

## Nutrient Uptake Forms
Common plant-available forms include:
- N as NO3- or NH4+
- P as H2PO4- or HPO4--
- K as K+
- Ca as Ca++
- Mg as Mg++
- S as SO4--

## Typical Fertigation Ranges
Typical fertigation concentration ranges reported in the chapter:
- Nitrogen: 50–200 mg/L
- Phosphorus: 10–50 mg/L
- Potassium: 100–250 mg/L
- Calcium: 50–150 mg/L
- Magnesium: 20–50 mg/L

Micronutrient ranges are much smaller and usually added only when needed based on analysis.

## Fertilization Principles
Balanced fertilization should be based on:
- crop nutrient demand
- soil nutrient reserves
- soil chemical analysis
- plant analysis when needed

Poor practice:
- applying fertilizer by rule of thumb
- using excessive N, P, and K
- applying all fertilizer too early
- not adjusting to soil reserves

Good practice:
- deduct soil reserves from crop requirement
- split applications through the season
- use fertigation where possible
- monitor nutrient status and adjust

## Environmental Risk of Overfertilization
Poor nutrient management can increase:
- nitrate leaching
- salinization
- eutrophication
- greenhouse gas emissions
- fertilizer waste

In open soilless systems, nutrient solution discharge can significantly increase groundwater pollution risk.

## Nitrogen (N)
Nitrogen is essential for:
- proteins
- enzymes
- chlorophyll
- nucleic acids
- vegetative growth
- flower and fruit formation

### Main N sources
- irrigation water
- soil organic matter
- crop residues
- animal manures
- compost
- fertilizers

### Important N transformations
- mineralization
- immobilization
- nitrification

### Main N loss routes
- volatilization
- denitrification
- leaching

### Practical N rules
- greenhouse growers should account for N in irrigation water
- surface-spread fertilizer is risky under warm, moist, high-pH conditions
- light incorporation reduces volatilization losses
- avoid waterlogging to reduce denitrification
- sandy soils are more prone to nitrate leaching

## Phosphorus (P)
Phosphorus is essential for:
- energy transfer
- photosynthesis
- respiration
- nucleic acid synthesis

### Important P notes
- plants take up P mainly in orthophosphate forms
- P is much less mobile in soil than nitrate
- P availability depends strongly on pH
- pH 6–7 generally supports better P use efficiency

### P fixation
- in alkaline soils, calcium tends to reduce P solubility
- in acidic soils, aluminium and iron tend to fix P
- greenhouse soils in warm climates can be strong P fixers

### Practical P rule
P usually does not leach much, but poor management can still increase phosphate in water passing through or over soil.

## Potassium (K)
Potassium is important for:
- water regulation
- photosynthesis
- plant health
- fruit quality
- firmness
- flavour
- soluble solids

### Important K notes
- K is highly mobile in the plant
- deficiency symptoms often start on older leaves
- excess K can restrict Ca and Mg uptake
- clay and organic soils hold K better than sandy soils
- sandy soils are more vulnerable to K loss

## Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Sulphur (S)
### Calcium
- important for cell walls and membranes
- deficiency appears first on new growth
- uptake depends strongly on transpiration and healthy roots
- excess NH4, Mg, or K can reduce Ca uptake

### Magnesium
- central component of chlorophyll
- mobile in the plant
- deficiency appears first on older leaves
- excess K, Ca, or NH4 can reduce Mg uptake

### Sulphur
- important in amino acids and enzymes
- absorbed mainly as sulphate
- deficiency appears in newer leaves
- generally less problematic because many fertilizers already contain sulphur

## Micronutrients
Micronutrients are required in small amounts but remain essential.

Important general rules:
- availability is strongly influenced by soil pH
- high pH often leads to Fe deficiency
- low pH may increase toxicity risk for some elements like Mn
- foliar correction may help when leaf analysis shows low levels

### Examples
- Fe deficiency: interveinal chlorosis on young leaves
- Mn deficiency: speckled interveinal chlorosis
- Zn deficiency: stunting and reduced leaf size
- B deficiency: growing-point damage and necrosis
- Mo deficiency: more likely under acidic conditions

## Seasonal Nutrient Demand
Nutrient uptake changes during the season according to:
- light
- temperature
- humidity
- crop stage
- fruiting status

Important rule:
- nutrient demand is not constant
- long-cycle crops like tomato show major seasonal variation
- overfeeding can lead to luxury consumption without yield benefit

## Core Best Practices
- test soil before the growing season
- consider soil pH, SOM, CEC, and texture
- base fertilizer plans on crop need and soil reserves
- split nutrient supply during the season
- use fertigation for precision
- avoid overirrigation and oversupply
- monitor visually and analytically
- treat nutrient management as both a productivity and environmental issue
