## Overview
Microirrigation refers to irrigation systems operating at low flow rates and low pressures, usually below about 1.5 to 2 bar, and delivering water close to the plant root zone. It is widely used in greenhouse crops because it supports efficient, localized watering and can be combined with fertigation.

## Main Components
A microirrigation system commonly includes:
- pump
- filtration system
- control valves
- delivery network
- emitters or discharge devices

The delivery system may include:
- mainlines
- submains
- drip lines
- emitters or micro-sprinkling devices

## Main Advantages
Microirrigation offers:
- high irrigation efficiency
- low runoff risk
- application of small water volumes over long periods
- reduced weed development
- lower air humidity around foliage compared with broad wetting systems
- reduced pathogen pressure in many cases
- low operating pressure and lower energy cost
- compatibility with fertigation

## Main Limitation
The most common technical problem is clogging of emitters or nozzles. Because of this, upstream filtration is essential.

## Main System Types
The chapter describes six main categories:
- drip lines
- systems with drippers
- systems with intermittent emitters
- systems with capillary tubes
- micro- or mini-sprinklers
- subsurface drip irrigation

## Drip Lines
Drip lines are common in row crops and greenhouse vegetables.

### Notes
- true drip lines regulate flow better than simple perforated hoses
- self-compensating drip lines are useful on sloping land or long distances
- self-compensating drippers help maintain a more stable flow despite pressure differences
- some self-compensating systems also include anti-clogging or self-washing behavior

## Systems with Drippers
These systems use polyethylene tubes with inserted drippers placed at crop-appropriate spacing.

### Useful notes
- button-type drippers provide controlled flow
- self-compensating models maintain more stable flow
- anti-drain drippers are useful in soilless crops with short, frequent irrigation cycles
- arrow-type drippers can be easier to inspect and clean

## Intermittent Emitters
These have higher unit flow rates than typical drippers and may reduce clogging risk while wetting a larger area.

## Capillary Tube Systems
These are relatively low-cost and can be delivered pre-assembled, but they may provide less uniform distribution because of pressure losses along the line. They are better suited to shorter lines or layouts designed to reduce pressure gradients.

## Micro- and Mini-Sprinklers
These systems combine some features of drip and sprinkler irrigation.

### Notes
- microsprinklers: lower flow range
- minisprinklers: higher flow range
- can wet wider areas than drippers
- can sometimes be used for frost protection if automated
- droplet size matters for frost protection efficiency

## Subsurface Drip Irrigation
Subsurface drip irrigation places drip hoses below the soil surface.

### Advantages
- reduced evaporation from the soil surface
- high water-use efficiency
- reduced foliage wetness and reduced weed growth
- improved fertigation close to roots
- soil remains workable during irrigation
- possible use with wastewater in some contexts

### Limitations
- cannot help with seed germination or early rooting
- higher cost
- clogging is harder to detect
- root intrusion may occur
- strong filtration is required

## Irrigation Efficiency
The chapter presents microirrigation as the most efficient of the common irrigation methods when systems are well designed and managed.

### General comparison
- surface irrigation: lowest efficiency
- sprinkler systems: moderate efficiency
- microirrigation: can reach about 90 to 95 percent in well-designed systems

## Conditions for High Efficiency
High efficiency depends on:
- good system design
- placing water close to plants
- frequent irrigation
- small volumes over longer delivery periods
- appropriate operating pressure
- good distribution uniformity

## Distribution Uniformity
Uneven flow between emitters is a major cause of inefficiency.

### Why it matters
Poor distribution uniformity causes:
- overirrigation in some areas
- underwatering in others
- deep percolation losses
- nutrient leaching
- uneven plant growth
- lower fertigation efficiency

### Practical interpretation
Distribution uniformity should be kept high, and emitters with low manufacturing variability are preferred.

## Clogging Risk
Clogging may result from:
- suspended solids
- precipitated salts
- bacteria and algae
- poor maintenance

### Main management rule
Filtration is essential and should be matched to:
- water quality
- emitter sensitivity
- flow rate
- particle type

### Common filter types
- hydrocyclone filters
- sand filters
- screen filters
- disc filters

### Chemical maintenance
When clogging risk comes from dissolved or biological causes, chemical treatment may be needed:
- chlorination
- acid treatment

## Water Localization and Deep Percolation
Microirrigation improves efficiency, but excessive localization or excessive applied volume can reduce performance.

### Main risks
- too little wetted volume around roots
- deep percolation below root depth
- water waste in sandy soils if irrigation events are too long

### Soil-related logic
- sandy soils need frequent, small irrigations
- clayey soils retain larger volumes in the upper profile
- wetting pattern depends on soil texture and emitter flow rate

## Agronomic Management Principles
Good management should aim to provide water gradually and frequently, keeping moisture in the active root zone relatively stable.

### Main recommendations
- avoid long irrigation intervals with large volumes
- use shorter intervals and lower volumes
- match irrigation volume to soil type and root depth
- use more emitters with lower unit flow in sandy soils
- in sandy soils, daily irrigation may be appropriate
- under high crop demand, splitting irrigation within the day may be beneficial

## Maintenance Principles
Routine maintenance should include:
- flushing lines with clear water after fertigation
- emptying drip lines regularly when appropriate
- repairing leaks, breaks, or slipped pipes quickly
- maintaining filter function continuously

## GAP-Style Practical Recommendations
- always include adequate filtration upstream
- use self-compensating drippers on slopes or long lines
- choose system type based on crop, cost, and pressure requirements
- improve efficiency by reducing emitter flow variation
- avoid overlocalization and deep percolation
- keep irrigation frequent and moderate rather than infrequent and excessive
