## Overview
Mediterranean greenhouses are generally low-cost plastic-covered structures without active climate control. They commonly use drip irrigation and are mainly used for vegetable production. In dry Mediterranean regions, water is scarce and greenhouse horticulture can contribute to overexploitation, nitrate contamination, and salinization of aquifers, so irrigation management must aim for both productivity and water-use efficiency.

## Core Principle
For greenhouse-grown crops, net crop water requirement can usually be treated as equal to crop evapotranspiration (ETc) because:
- rainfall does not enter the greenhouse
- seasonal soil water extraction is usually negligible
- soils are often kept near field capacity with high-frequency drip irrigation

Gross crop water requirement is higher than net requirement because it also considers salinity and irrigation uniformity.

## Seasonal Water Requirement Ranges
Experimentally measured ETc values for major greenhouse vegetables in Mediterranean conditions were reported in the range of about 170 to 371 mm per crop cycle.

Examples:
- sweet pepper in soil: up to 371 mm
- tomato in soil: about 231 to 260 mm depending on cycle
- melon in soil: about 177 to 219 mm
- watermelon in soil: about 170 mm
- substrate-grown tomato: about 177 to 276 mm

## Why Greenhouse Water Demand Is Lower Than Open Field
Compared with equivalent crops grown outdoors, greenhouse crops usually have lower evapotranspiration because:
- solar radiation is reduced
- wind speed inside the greenhouse is very low
- greenhouse evaporative demand may be around 60 percent of outside conditions
- sand mulch and protected conditions reduce direct soil evaporation

## Cooling and Radiation Effects
Whitewash applied to greenhouse covers during warm periods reduces solar radiation entering the greenhouse and lowers crop evapotranspiration. This means irrigation demand depends not only on crop type and stage, but also on greenhouse transmissivity and cooling practices.

## ETc Estimation Approach
The chapter explains that ETc can be estimated using the FAO approach:

ETc = ETo × Kc

Where:
- ETo = reference evapotranspiration driven by climate
- Kc = crop coefficient driven by crop species, structure, and growth stage

For Mediterranean greenhouses, practical estimation can use:
- adapted Penman-Monteith
- radiation-based methods
- historical climate data
- greenhouse transmissivity values

## Crop Coefficient Notes
Initial Kc values for transplanted seedlings are often around 0.2 in greenhouse vegetables.
Maximum Kc values are higher in vertically supported greenhouse crops such as tomato and pepper than in many open-field systems because of:
- taller crop structure
- more open canopy
- pruning
- improved light penetration

Typical supported-crop maximum Kc values reported:
- sweet pepper: about 1.3
- tomato: about 1.4
- melon (supported): about 1.3
- cucumber: about 1.2
- green beans: about 1.4

## Soil-Grown Crop Irrigation Practices
In Mediterranean greenhouses, soil-grown crops are commonly irrigated with drip irrigation.
Typical irrigation frequency:
- every day under warm conditions
- every 3 to 4 days under cooler conditions

Growers often use fixed schedules adapted according to crop performance and climate, but the chapter notes that there is room to improve irrigation efficiency.

## Relative Irrigation Supply
The ratio of applied irrigation to crop water requirement is called relative irrigation supply (RIS). Reported RIS values varied substantially between crops and farms, suggesting that some commercial irrigation practices apply more water than necessary, especially during crop establishment.

## Supplementary Irrigation Uses
Additional irrigation may also be applied:
- between crops for soil disinfection
- before transplanting to moisten the soil

These extra irrigations increase annual water use beyond crop ETc alone.

## Total Annual Water Use
For a regional case study in Almería, average total annual water use associated with greenhouse cropping was reported around 495 mm when considering:
- crop irrigation
- substrate drainage requirements
- soil disinfection irrigation
- pre-transplant irrigation

## Irrigation Scheduling for Soil-Grown Crops
A practical irrigation plan can be based on daily ETc estimates, supported by soil sensors.

Recommended combined approach:
- estimate daily ETc
- use soil water sensors, especially tensiometers, to decide irrigation timing and adjust frequency

The chapter presents tensiometers as especially useful for greenhouse vegetable crops in soil because they are:
- low cost
- simple
- reliable
- not affected by salinity
- well suited to moist greenhouse soils

## General Soil Sensor Guidance
Soil water sensors are used by defining:
- a lower limit, indicating when to start irrigation
- an upper limit, indicating when to stop irrigation

General matric potential ranges suggested for greenhouse vegetables:
- coarse soils: about -10 to -20 kPa
- medium soils: about -10 to -30 kPa
- fine soils: about -20 to -40 kPa

## Substrate-Grown Crop Irrigation
Substrate-grown crops need more precise irrigation than soil-grown crops because:
- root volume is smaller
- water-holding capacity is limited
- nutrients are less buffered

Management principles:
- use small, frequent irrigations
- maintain a leaching fraction of about 20 to 40 percent to prevent salt accumulation
- monitor drainage and salinity carefully

Common methods include:
- water level sensors
- soil water sensors suitable for substrates
- radiation-based triggering methods
- transpiration models

## Water-Use Efficiency
Greenhouse vegetable crops generally have higher irrigation water-use efficiency than open-field crops because:
- evaporative demand is lower
- productivity is higher
- crop value is higher

Reported tomato water-use efficiency values in unheated Mediterranean greenhouses were substantially higher than many open-field systems.
